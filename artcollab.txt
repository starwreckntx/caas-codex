There isn't a single simple equation, but the pattern of the "eyes" (called ocelli) on the peacock's train feathers relates to principles of fractals and pattern formation in nature.

Think of it like a crystal growing. A simple rule is repeated over and over, creating a complex and beautiful structure. In the peacock's feather, a basic set of instructions in its DNA tells the cells how to produce pigments in a specific sequence, creating the concentric rings of the ocellus. While not a perfect Fibonacci spiral, the arrangement and spacing of the barbs and barbules that form the feather are incredibly precise and repetitive, creating the overall pattern.

The "equation" is less about a formula you'd write down and more about a developmental algorithm:

Start Point: A central point is established on the developing feather.

Rule 1: Deposit a specific pigment (e.g., melanin for black).

Rule 2: At a set distance from the center, switch to a different instruction (e.g., create the blue structural color).

Rule 3: At another set distance, switch again (e.g., create the bronze/gold color).
This process repeats for each ocellus, creating that stunning, hypnotic pattern across the entire train.

The Physics of Iridescent Color
This is the coolest part and directly answers your question about the purple-green color. You can't mix a single paint to create it, because it's not a pigment. It's structural color.

Imagine a soap bubble or an oil slick on water. You see shimmering rainbows of color, right? But soap and oil aren't made of rainbow pigment. The color comes from light interacting with the incredibly thin layers of the material. This is called thin-film interference.

A peacock's feather works the same way. The feather's barbules contain microscopic, rod-like structures made of melanin and keratin, arranged in precise layers.  When light waves hit these layers:

Some waves reflect off the top surface.

Some waves pass through and reflect off the lower layers.

These reflecting waves interfere with each other. Depending on the thickness of the layers and the angle you're viewing from, certain colors (wavelengths) are canceled out, while others are reinforced and reflected back to your eye.

Green/Blue: Comes from a more ordered, grid-like arrangement of these keratin rods.

Purple/Bronze: Comes from slightly different spacing and layering.

Brown/Black: Comes from simple melanin pigment, which absorbs light.

So, that shimmering green-to-purple effect is literally the color of light itself, filtered and reflected by the feather's physical structure.

How to Paint the Iridescence (The Artist's Trick)
Since you can't create structural color with regular paint, you have to simulate the effect. Here are the best techniques to capture that iridescent look.

Start with a Dark Base: The most brilliant iridescence in nature appears over a dark background (usually melanin). Start your ocellus area with a dark, rich color like Payne's Gray, Indigo, or a deep Prussian Blue mixed with black. This will make the subsequent colors pop.

Layering is Key: Build your colors in thin layers (glazes).

Lay down a metallic bronze or gold color for the outer ring.

Follow with a layer of Phthalo Blue or Ultramarine.

Use a vibrant Phthalo Green or Viridian for the center.

Use Special Paints (Optional but effective):

Interference Paints: These are fantastic for this effect. They are semi-transparent and contain reflective flakes (like mica). An "Interference Violet" paint will look milky in the tube but will appear as a shimmering violet when painted over a dark color.

Color-Shift Paints: These are even more dramatic and will shift between two or more colors (like green-to-purple) depending on the viewing angle.

The Color Pattern of the "Eye":
If you break down a classic ocellus, the pattern usually goes from the center outward:

Center Spot: A dark, velvety patch of black or deep violet.

First Ring: A brilliant, metallic sapphire or ultramarine blue.

Second Ring: A vibrant, shimmering emerald or phthalo green.

Third Ring: A rich, metallic bronze or coppery gold.

Outer Fringe: Hazy, earthy tones of brown and dark green that blend into the rest of the feather.

# You will need to install a library to make this work.
# In your terminal or command prompt, type: pip install pyautogui

import pyautogui
import time

def capture_sequence(screenshots_to_take=5, delay_between_shots=10):
    """
    Captures a series of screenshots with a delay.

    Args:
        screenshots_to_take (int): The total number of screenshots to capture.
        delay_between_shots (int): The time in seconds to wait between each shot.
    """
    print("Starting screenshot sequence...")
    print(f"A screenshot will be taken every {delay_between_shots} seconds.")
    print(f"Total screenshots: {screenshots_to_take}")
    
    # Loop to take screenshots
    for i in range(screenshots_to_take):
        # Countdown for the user
        print(f"\nTaking screenshot {i + 1} of {screenshots_to_take} in:")
        for j in range(delay_between_shots, 0, -1):
            print(f"...{j}", end="\r")
            time.sleep(1)
        
        # Define the filename for the screenshot
        filename = f"live_capture_{i + 1}.png"
        
        # Capture the screenshot
        screenshot = pyautogui.screenshot()
        
        # Save the screenshot
        screenshot.save(filename)
        
        print(f"\nScreenshot saved as {filename}")

    print("\nSequence complete. All screenshots have been saved.")

# --- Run the protocol ---

# For example, to take 10 screenshots with a 5-second delay:

capture_sequence(5, 10)
